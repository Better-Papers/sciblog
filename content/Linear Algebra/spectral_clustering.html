Graph Theory

Definition 1 (undirected graph) In a simple graph, the graph is undirected, no loops, no weights and is defined by \[ G(V, E, w) \] where \(V\) is a set of vertices and E is a set of two-sets (sets with two distinct elements) of vertices, whose elements are called edges, and \(w\) is a function \(w: E \rightarrow \mathcal{R}^+\).
Definition 2 (adjacency matrix) An adjacency matrix describes the connections within the graph. \[ A (i,j) = \begin{cases} w(i,j) & (i,j) \in E \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \] The matrix is hollow, since thereâ€™s no self-loop.
Definition 3 (degree matrix) \[ D = \mathrm{diag} \pp{\sum_j A(i,j)} \]

Definition 4 (Laplacian matrix) In a simple graph, the Laplacian matrix is an \(|V| \times |V|\) matrix defined as \[ L = D-A \] where \(D\) is the degree matrix and \(A\) is the adjacency matrix of the graph.

Let \(A_i\) be the column of an adjacency matrix, then \[ L = \sum_{(i,j)\in E} (A_i - A_j)(A_i - A_j)^\top \] That is, summing over all columns of each edge elements.

As an example, consider a simple graph with two vertices and one edge. Then
